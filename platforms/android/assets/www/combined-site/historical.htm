Sundial's Historical Sites

The Sun always Rises in the East and Sets in the West.

But the time and angle in the sky of the Sun changes through the seasons.

The Sun "gets stuck" at the same point on the horizon for a week at the Solstices. This angle is what is tracked by ancient observatories like Stonehenge and Goseck Circle dating back to 4900 B.C. This time when the Sun got "stuck" must have been alarming to pre-Copernican people.

According to The Discovery of Middle Earth, the Heraklean Way was mapped across Europe using the same Solstice angle of the Sun:
. The Heraklean Way goes through Andora, Coeals, Muntaner, Ordino, Marsailles, Matromona Pass (57.53°), Norbonne (56.28°), Nimes, Briançon, Deneuvre, Agatha, Sacred Promitory and Matrimona Pass.
Many roads in Europe, UK and Ireland prefectly align with the angle of the Sun at the Solstices. The Celts and Druids used Tan(11÷7)=56.53° as their Solstice angle. England (and the world) currently use Tan(4÷3)53.13°. The Pagans in Ireland used Tan(9÷5)=57.96° to 59.94°.

There as many types of historical sundials, including the Dial of Ahaz which is described in The Bible, see Isaiah 18:8 and 2 Kings 20:11. The Galations of The Bible were named after Celts from Gaul in North-West France, which used to be called "Celtica".

These people used Sundials: Anaximander of Miletus, Alexander the Great, Eucllid, Shakespeare mentions Sundials in As You Like It, Act 2, scene 3.
